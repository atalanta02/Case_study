## Timeline of key events 
** Before 1940**
After recognizing larger numbers but still used digits from 0 to 9. Businessmen from China, Turkey and Greece used the abacus (abacus) to perform the calculations.  In 1617, John Napier came up with logarithmic calculations and invented a tool called the Napier bone. Blaise Pascal invented the first mechanical calculation machine in 1642.  This machine operates by moving the gears on the wheels. 
And the beginning of the actual computer was formed by an English professor of mathematics, Charles Babbage (1791-1871). In 1812, Babbage noticed how machines were excellent at solving recuring mathematical problems. Babbage's first machine was called the Differential Machine in 1822 when he proposed a machine for calculating differential equations. Using steam power, the machine can store programs, can perform calculations and print the results automatically, can solve mathematical calculation problems such as logarithms mechanically precisely up to twenty digits. After working with the Differential Machine for ten years, Babbage was suddenly inspired to start building the first general-purpose computer, called the Analytical Engine. Babbage's assistant, Augusta Ada King (1815-1842) had an important role in the creation of this machine. He helped revise plans, seek funding from the UK government, and communicate the Analytical Engine's specifications to the public. In addition, Augusta's good understanding of the machine allowed her to create instructions to be put into the machine and also made her the first female programmer. In 1980, the United States Department of Defense named a programming language ADA in his honor. In 1889, Herman Hollerith (1860-1929) also applied the principle of perforation cards to perform calculations. His first task was to find a faster way to do the calculations for the United States Census Bureau. The previous census conducted in 1880 took seven years to complete the calculation. With the population growing, the Bureau estimates that it will take ten years to complete the census calculations. In the following days, several engineers made other new inventions. Vannevar Bush (1890-1974) created a calculator to solve differential equations in 1931. The machine can solve complex differential equations that have been considered complicated by academics. The machine was very large and heavy due to the hundreds of cleats and shafts needed to perform the calculations. 
In 1903, John V. Atanasoff and Clifford Berry attempted to create an electric computer that applied Boolean algebra to electrical circuits. This approach is based on the work of George Boole (1815-1864) in the form of the binary algebraic system, which states that any mathematical equation can be declared as true or false. Howard Aiken introduced the use of an electromechanical machine called the Mark I in 1937. It is large and heavy in shape and contains a long wire cable. All operations inside the computer are carried out by electromagnetic energy. By applying true-false conditions into electrical circuits in a disconnected form, Atanasoff and Berry created the first electric computer in 1940. However, their project was halted due to a lack of funding sources.
